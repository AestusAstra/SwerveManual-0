DESIGN Requirements - A CADer with a decent amount of exerpience, and a Programmer with a decent amount of experience CAD - Onshape, Fusion360, Solidworks all work. However, Onshape is reccomended with its wide availability of featurescripts and plugins as well as its parts library. simbas Cad lib ftc catalog part lighten beltgen FUNDAMENTAL CONSIDERATIONS COG and Mass distribution - in order to maximize speed and minimize current draw while turning, mass should be located closest to the center of the robot from a top- down view. One should also try to make the center of gravity as low as possible in order to avoid tipping or in games where there is a lot of extension, lurch. Breakage - Robots will hit each other, and robots will break. Attempt to 1. Avoid breakage by making things well supported, out of the right materials, and parts, avoid stressing actuators, and protect them via adding an independently supported output to prevent shock loads and more. drivetrain optimization - make sure that the gear ratio makes sense given the weight of your robot and your goals - having an overagressive ratio for your weight will lead to brownouts and blown fuses, or otherwise sluggish robot with significant current draw, while a less agressive one might lead to a lack of speed. mounting - you need to be able to attatch things bruh Belts Belt wrap - gt2 belts tend not to do well with wrap around pulleys with <20 teeth Slot EVERYTHING YOU CAN FOR BELTS. BELTS WILL EXPAND AND STRETCH. use gt2 belts for high rpm, low torque use 5mm HTD for low rpm, high torque 3mm HTD for in between. BEARINGS - bearings are often expensive, especially the large x contacts for the pods. find good sealed bearings and dont mess them up. They can be bought for slightly cheaper on amazon (uxcell) and you can often find them on thriftybot. Swerve Specific - Wheel thickness - as tempting as it may be to slap on a 10 mm wheel, the thickness of the wheel plays a heavy role in the overall traction of the drivetrain. aim for >14mm - wheel durometer - between 20 to 70 durometer appears to be the sweet spot. while the best option is custom molded polyurethane with treads perpendicular to the direction of motion, gray grippy tread is available for cheap and is very quick and easy to install(super glue it to the wheel). polyurethane might take experimentation and so should only be done with a backup ready. Coax effect - a killer for programming for many rookie teams. Make the ratio between the motor driven pulley and the motor drive pulley for wheel rotation as small as possible, and put most of the reduction in the gearing in the pod, as a large ratio will create a strong coaxial effect upon the shaft, and lead to a weak rotation of the pod while the wheel rotates. This means the servo rotating the pod will experience an uneven force, and programming will be near impossible. anything over a ratio of 2:1 is unworkable. Pod rotation pulleys will often skip. this is usually due to a combination of these factors robot weight - the robot is too heavy. lack of tension - always try to find perfect tension via a slotted test rig for your pod rotation pulleys and belts first before making the final cad. tensioners can cause uneven forces and cause issues with programming lack of belt engagement - just make the pod rotation pulleys bigger. lol. it is also possible to just use a gear for the pod rotation if pulleys seem too problematic. K I S S keep it stupid simple - try to reduce complexity wherever possible. Uneccesary power transmissions, features and more should be eliminated. however, keep in mind that we are optimizing for performance. Dont simplify things for the sake of simplicity, otherwise you might end up cutting essential features. Remember what the goal of what youre doing is - if your changes don't acheive a particular goal, then don't do it. Make it compact - but not too compact. Swervedrives still need some space to be effective. Being too small can lead to a lack of traction with wheel thicknesses being too low, or fragility. The point of compactness is to improve COG- center of gravity, PROGRAMMING - swervekinematics can be found on Chief Delphi if you want to code yours from scratch, it is reccomended you do so in the offseason, as coding swerve from scratch is pretty hard. overall, the architecture goes like this COAX each module has an axon, with a "polarity" value. you can set your axis however you want. In this example I will use the typical quandrant setup the "top right" or "front right" will be 1,1, the bottom left will be -1, -1. the top right axon will turn to a set angle, calculated by the x and y values of your left joystick, which will determine the overall direction of motion of the robot. each other axon will turn to a set angle, with an offset based on its quandrant. for example, -1,-1 will have it be offset by 180 degrees. if you are using swerve mode axons (unprogrammed mk2 minis) then they will not require a PID, as they will be registered in software as servo mode with a built in PID. if you are using CR mode with 4th wire encoders, use a pid, although typically only p is neccesary. keep p values VERY LOW at first while tuning as it is extremely sensitive. cooking an axon is like losing 90 dollars. try like 0.0005 motor velocity can be also set with a separate pid for more stable driving but is not neccesary, and it will require more encoders. congrats! you're a third of the way there! next, comes rotation.
